Tokyo Trip

Day 1 & 2: Tokyo

Since Tokyo has the largest international airport in Japan, you will probably start your trip in Tokyo, and if you are traveling for 7 days, you should immediately activate your JR Pass to take advantage of the free JR trains that run through the city. While you could spend a week in Tokyo and never get bored, here are a few things to see and do.

Visit the fish market

Toyosu is the largest fish market in the world. This daily auction determines much of the world's sushi supply. You can visit the market for free, but a food and beverage tour of the Tsukiji Outside Market sells for about 13,500 yen.

See Sensoji Temple

Senso-ji Temple is beautifully painted and stands in a scenic location near the five-story pagoda and the famous Kaminarimon gate. Inside the main hall is a huge statue of the Kannon (Goddess of Mercy). It is always crowded, but it is worth seeing for yourself.

Drink in Golden Gai

This alley lined with back alley drinking establishments is lively at night and has a bit of a red-light district feel to it. It is not to be missed. Even if you don't drink, be sure to stop by and have a wander.

Visit the Imperial Palace

Built in the 15th century, this was the residence of the Japanese emperor, and although you cannot enter, the palace and its grounds are a peaceful place for a stroll.

Watch a sumo match

If you are in town at the right time, this is a must-see. Tickets sell out quickly, so book early; expect to pay around ¥3,800.

Days 3 & 4: Kyoto

Kyoto is without a doubt the most beautiful city in Japan. It is like stepping back in time. It is surrounded by mountains and dotted with temples, gardens, and bamboo groves. However, because of its beauty, it attracts many visitors, so it is best to visit outside of the busy summer season. But even with all the tourists, the city is majestic and full of many attractions. Some things to see and do that you shouldn’t miss are the following:

Visit the Golden Pavillion

This famous (and picturesque) temple was built in the 1950s when a monk burned down the previous temple (14th century) in an attempt to commit suicide. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most visited tourist destinations in the country.

Explore Gion

Gion is a historic geisha district. Walking along the main street, you can see ochaya (tea houses where geisha entertain guests), small stores, and many restaurants. 1,600 yen will get you a walking tour of Gion.

Wander in the Bamboo Forest

For a more relaxing experience, head to Arashiyama and be enveloped by the gentle swaying of the forest. Located near the famous Tenryuji Temple, it is one of the most beautiful places in the country (arrive early if you want to avoid the crowds).

Admire Ryoan-ji temple

This is my favorite temple in Kyoto. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is home to a mausoleum containing the remains of seven emperors. The traditional rock and sand garden is considered one of the best in the country.

Day 5 & 7: Osaka

Osaka is the third largest city in Japan. Although it is a financial center, I come here to enjoy the food. Sushi, sashimi, Kobe beef, yakiniku, ramen, and many other foods are available in Osaka. And then there are the specialties: okonomiyaki, kushikatsu, etc. You can take a food tour for about 12,000 yen or just wander around and eat. That's all I do: eat, eat, eat. But don't miss Osaka Castle. Osaka Castle was built in 1931 and is not original, but it is still an impressive sight. There is a small but insightful museum, and the observatory offers a picturesque view of the city. The main street of Dotonbori is lined with restaurants and stores, with lots of neon lights and signs (ideal at night).
